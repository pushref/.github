Geomatics in 2026

Fifteen years ago, a geomatics professional was first and foremost a cartographer. Today, that role has nearly disappeared — not because there are no more maps to make, but because maps are no longer the core of the work.

The modern geomatics professional is a geographic data engineer. They code in Python, structure PostGIS databases, deploy automated processing pipelines, integrate deep learning model outputs, and discuss data architecture with IT directors. A hybrid profession halfway between developer, data analyst, and territorial expert.

And this profession just took the biggest jolt of its recent history: the arrival of Earth observation foundation models.

In less than three years, the landscape has shifted. Supervised classification of satellite images — which used to require weeks of manual labeling, training a Random Forest, and validation — can now be reduced to a few hours of fine-tuning on a pre-trained model.

But as always when a profession takes a jolt, two traps emerge.

  • Fascination. "We'll automate everything." As if geographic expertise no longer mattered.
  • Retreat. "AI is hype, we keep doing things the old way." As if nothing changed.

AIPushTech exists to help professionals — who don't have time to read ten newsletters a week, let alone ArXiv papers — navigate between these two traps. Seven pages to set the stage.

  • The "no-code" illusion. Esri AI solutions or QGIS plugins promise magic, but production workflows require control you only get with code.

Understand foundation models (Prithvi, TerraMind, Clay, AlphaEarth) without having to read ArXiv papers. Identify AI use cases in geomatics that genuinely work vs. those that are marketing. Stay technically ahead of peers. Preserve their role as domain expert against the arrival of "generalist data scientists."

[ 03 · 2020-2026 TRAJECTORY ] PUSHREF · DOMAIN GIS-GEOMATICS

What AI has changed

To understand where we are in 2026, let's briefly trace the last five years.

  • 2020-2022 Classification from scratch Random Forests or convolutional networks trained from scratch on manually labeled datasets. Robust but expensive: 1,000-10,000 annotated images per task. Reference tools: scikit-learn, eo-learn, custom notebooks.
  • AUGUST 2023 Prithvi-EO 1.0 (NASA + IBM) First truly open and usable geospatial foundation model. 100M parameters, MAE-pretrained on 1TB of HLS imagery. Four initial use cases: flood mapping, burn scar detection, cloud gap imputation, crop segmentation.
  • DECEMBER 2024 Prithvi-EO 2.0 — 6× larger 600M parameters, 4.2 million samples. 75.6% on GEO-Bench, 8 points above. The ecosystem densifies: Clay Foundation Model, Microsoft AI for Good, Esri integrates AI into ArcGIS Pro.
  • 2025 TerraMind (IBM + ESA) Multimodal model of unprecedented ambition. 9 simultaneous modalities: optical, SAR, elevation, weather, textual annotations. Can generate missing modalities. A step toward conversational geospatial assistants.
  • 2026 AlphaEarth, Geospatial Reasoning, ArcGIS AI 4.0 Querying a map happens in plain English. Fine-tuning on your area takes a few GPU hours. Temporal change analysis over 10-year archives runs on a laptop with an RTX 4080.

Five practical transformations for Camille

  • Supervised classification cost collapses. 200 images often suffice where 5,000 were needed.
  • Multimodal analysis becomes native. Combining optical + SAR + elevation is no longer an integration problem but an API question.
  • Natural language enters the chain. "Show me building changes in this town" becomes an executable query.
  • Entry barriers drop for smaller local governments. Hugging Face lets a mid-sized metropolitan area match the quality only large players could deliver before.
  • The geomatician/data scientist line blurs. Camille, who codes, naturally moves toward mastering these tools.

[ 04 · WHAT DOESN'T CHANGE ] PUSHREF · DOMAIN GIS-GEOMATICS

What doesn't change

An honest founder's note must also state what does not change. Because many things don't change, and Camille needs to know it.

Reference system rigor remains intact

If you mix Lambert 93 and WGS84 in a classification script, your model can be perfect but your output will be off by several meters on the ground. No AI fixes this. Knowledge of CRS, EPSG, transformations between systems remains a non-negotiable skill.

Training data quality remains critical

AI doesn't invent quality. If your training dataset is biased, your model inherits the biases. The ability to build a quality dataset has become more important than the ability to train a model.

Domain expertise remains irreplaceable

Understanding that an anomalous signal on an orthophoto is not a built-environment change but seasonal vegetation variation requires geographic expertise. No foundation model knows this natively.

Manual validation remains mandatory

For most local government use cases (cadastre, PLU, ZAN), an AI result is never published without human review. Hallucinations exist in remote sensing as in LLMs.

Communication with the business remains critical

Explaining to an elected official why the model detected 1,200 new buildings when 800 were expected, what to make of it, how to cross-reference with building permits — that's the geomatician's job, not the model's.

AI accelerates, shifts, transforms. But it doesn't replace reference rigor, geographic expertise, validation, or business translation.

The good geomatics professional in 2026 is the one who combines new AI tools with these unchanging fundamentals.

Why this domain matters

Three reasons this domain particularly matters, and why Camille deserves better than yet another newsletter.

1. The profession is in deep transformation, and no one is telling it clearly

Commercial publishers talk about their tools. Researchers talk about their papers. Institutions talk about their programs. But no one synthesizes for the practitioner — "here's what concretely changes in your job, this month". AIPushTech fills this gap.

2. France and Europe have a world-class geomatics ecosystem, under-mediatized

IGN, CNES, Cerema, AFIGÉO, Géoplateforme, regional CRIGE — this whole ecosystem produces, publishes, organizes. But media attention goes preferentially to US actors. Pushref, a French editorial brand, makes the opposite editorial choice: giving French and European sources the visibility they deserve, by putting them in dialogue with the global ecosystem.

3. Geomatics is one of the professions where AI has the most tangible and least-discussed impact

Everyone talks about ChatGPT and marketing. No one really talks about what Prithvi will change in French local government GIS departments over the next 24 months. It's precisely this zone — deep professional transformations, under-mediatized — that Pushref is designed to cover.
